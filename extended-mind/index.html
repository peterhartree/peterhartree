Extended mind design [DRAFT]

Feb 5, 2016

Things outside the skull play an important role in our cognition.

Notebooks aid information storage and retrieval. Pen and paper supplement our working memory. Post-it notes bring things to our attention. Environmental cues trigger automatic patterns of behaviour.

We all extend our minds in ways like these. I think most people don’t think about this enough.

You can benefit if you take a considered approach to design, build and refine your extended mind. This post offers a brief survey of the terrain.

Why take this perspective?

The extended mind perspective encourages us to think in terms of outsourcing cognitive process and function. This helps us develop a coherent strategy for engaging with techniques and tools commonly presented under rubrics of “productivity advice”, “lifehacking” or “metaskills".

For the philosophers: the extended mind perspective is a useful model, whether or not you think functionalism and the extended mind thesis are true in some stronger sense than that.

Why care?

A good external mind lets you free up scarce internal cognitive resources. With skill (that's another essay) you can then reallocate these resources to the things that most merit your attention. A good external mind will also improve "your" performance on the surprisingly wide range of tasks which can be partially or fully externalised.

Designing your extended mind

Practical implementations of extended mind systems vary greatly.

I will refer to the collection of software and hardware that support the system as an extended mind "stack". The configuration of these components, and the way they relate to each other, I call "architecture".

Some desirable qualities for any extended mind system [1]:

  • Low friction: transfer from “internal” to “external” should be cheap and effortless.
  • Accessible: external resources should always be easily available when needed.
  • Reliable: external resources should perform as expected and be backed up safely.
  • Trustworthy: information retrieved from external resources should be more-or-less automatically endorsed. It should be deemed about as trustworthy as something retrieved clearly from biological memory.
  • Secure: external resources should have robust access control, so that sensitive information can be stored or manipulated externally without hestitation.
  • Simple: minimise components and interfaces.
  • Transparent: it should be easy to understand how the system works, to detect failures, and to debug and make changes when necessary.

The boundary between extended mind and world

When should we think of something as part of our extended mind? Are books, for example, part of our long term memory? In their classic paper, Clark and Chalmers (1998) say we should include any part of the world that performs a process which, if it were done in the head, we would call a cognitive process. Since then, more restrictive criteria have been suggested (c.f. Smart (forthcoming), pp.3-4). My preferred rule of thumb is:

if it's doing something my brain could have done, and it's normally less than 10 seconds away from my access or control, then it's part of my extended mind.

Closing thoughts

A well designed external mind system seems like an increasingly valuable asset. Yet, in 2016, it's quite difficult for an individual to design and implement such a system. One reason for this is that, right now, the cloud software ecosystem is a mess of (excellent) narrow solutions which must be taped together using connector services and a great deal of trial, error and headscratching.
